Introduction

One of the most influential driving forces for the wellbeing of the restaurant industry, as well as the eating habits of the general public in the recent decade has been the advent of food-focused smartphone applications. Any time the name of a restaurant, or even cuisine, is entered into a search engine, the reviews provided and collected by companies such as Zomato and Google often follow or even rank higher than the website of the restaurant itself in the displayed results. As such, it is clear that search engines and third party applications play a huge factor in the success of a restaurant - in fact, customers are 87% likely to perform a Google search to discover restaurants near them as opposed to using other sources (Clampet, 2019). At the same time, due to this increased exposure, the offerings of individual restaurants are on greater display than ever, allowing the consumer to make more informed choices based on the metrics which they value. According to a State of the Industry report by the National Restaurant Association (2017), '70 percent of consumers said availability of healthy menu items influences where they dine out'. Furthermore, 74% of individuals self reported as aged 18 to 36 chose specific restaurants over similar restaurants due to the availability of healthy options (ibid, 2017). As such, there is no doubt that the trend of making healthier food choices will continue growing in popularity and as such, present opportunities for new entries into the field of applications to assist users in finding these options.

There are many factors that could influence a person’s diet, a major one being the choice or ability to eat out instead of preparing food at home. This is often seen as an indulgent choice; however, people may not always have the choice to prepare food as the pace of modern life leaves a lot of people under immense time pressure. Food when eating out is usually more expensive; in addition, the diner cannot control what goes into their food, so they lack as much agency to choose healthier ingredients or add less oil, etc. To add to this, less healthy options such as fast food are often less expensive and are marketed as such, meaning a person’s socioeconomic status also plays a role in what food they are likely to choose when eating out. Indeed, in first world countries, low socioeconomic status has been found to be correlated with higher chances of obesity (McLaren, 2007). As such, there are always members of the public seeking to make a positive change to their diet, and it is for this reason that we have chosen a healthy eating app to be the topic of our report.

This project revolves around designing an app specifically for people who attend RMIT University to make them aware of and assist them in finding healthier food options around their campus. A major challenge we foresee is the brand loyalty of users to already established food apps as well as search engines - the loyalty of their users and intersection between users of rival apps is not clear. Therefore, a major problem identified is that the suitability and catering of the apps compared to established food apps and search engines in general that do not really cater to any one specific user-base anymore, and take a much more generalist approach in their audience. Another issue is the development timeline - we are banking on the fact that the trend in health-conscious eating will continue as the app goes through its development cycle, as the data from our research relates to current and past trends. Finally, a major issue that will be investigated heavily in the survey is the inclusions and usage of features of the app that already have established alternatives, such as maps which already has an established competitor in the form of Google Maps. Will users still be open to using our app despite the existence of an established competitor?

Proposed solution

According to Schumer, Amadi & Joshi (2018), the rating of a health food related app is higher when labeled as a 'diet' app. Our app would share features of these same apps as well as those found in traditional dining apps such as Zomato. This would allow eating out to work within the confines as to what a diet is traditionally viewed, with recognisable features from diet apps seamlessly integrated with those of a dining out application (e.g a calorie counter that counts the calories based on menu selection.)

What are the main tasks the users are expected to accomplish in these competitors?

Zomato is a food discovery app that facilitates the user’s ability to find and make informed decisions as to locations at which to dine out. The app uses the location of the device, as well as the reviews of restaurants they have previously visited, to suggest new restaurants for the user to try. It is also possible to easily search these results either by keyword or by cuisine.

The main tasks users are expected to accomplish using Zomato are:

  • Finding and displaying highly rated restaurants located near the user
  • Being able to search for specific cuisines as well as keywords
  • Seeing updated user reviews and menus to be able to make an informed decision
  • Being able to make reservations on the app
  • Getting an approximate idea of the cost per head, as well as being able to view the menu, before committing to the restaurant

The layout of the app is very easy to work with. Upon startup, a variety of local 4+ star restaurants is immediately displayed to the user in the form of a scrollable list with large images of the food offered at each venue. As the suggestions are drawn from the user’s usage data, the suggestions will become more tailored to the user the more they use the app.

It is extremely simple to use make a reservation from inside the app, as your details are collected at the moment you log in with an account, whether it be a Zomato account, Google or Facebook. This circumvents the need to make a phone call to the restaurant, saving a lot of hassle for both the user and the restaurant. This is an extremely streamlined and user friendly experience, taking only 5 taps to book a table from the main ‘discover’ screen.

On top of this, Zomato also allows the user to sort restaurants by the specific cuisine in which they are interested. Selecting one of the categories under the cuisines page will take the user to a results page similar to the search function, but with the ability to further narrow down their selection via a search bar. Zomato also offers an ‘events’ page, which displays special events and deals currently on offer at local restaurants. All of these sections are all well designed and easy to navigate, however the sheer magnitude of features on offer in the app means that the top scroll bar appears a little cluttered, which could impact the ease of use and intuitiveness of the app, especially among infrequent or inexperienced users.

One strength of the Zomato platform is the depth of the information about each restaurant. Users can submit images of the food on offer, as well as images of the menu pages in order for potential customers to make decisions without having to actually visit. A detailed list of features and facilities provided at each restaurant, as well as an average cost per head, is also displayed for each restaurant on top of the traditional star review system. The app is overall very well designed and easy to use.

Part 2: Survey and design discussion

Pre-survey methodology

The survey questions were initially designed to establish the user groups by age, whether they were a student, and the frequency at which they eat out. The responses to the initial questions would provide a clearer understanding of the demographics of users, how many intersecting attributes they shared, and perhaps even a deeper understanding of the type of people that would eat on-campus. The next section revolves around the topic of food apps. Specifically, the respondents are questioned as to whether they have used Yelp and Zomato, two of our biggest competitors. If so, the questions address specific features of said apps they used (i.e food reviews) and those they did not (i.e in-app directions), followed by their use frequency, and their motivation for downloading them. Finally, a section dedicated to certain healthy eating habits often assisted by apps, such as whether the respondents logged meals, was added. This would help us identify whether our target audience values the core features proposed for our app.

Survey methodology

The survey was constructed in Google Forms and sent to various social groups via Facebook Messenger and Discord over a period of a week. A total of 16 responses were received. The Participant Information Form was included at the start of the survey in order to inform the participants about the scope and methodology of the survey.

Observation methodology

As we are also part of one of the target user groups, that being students at RMIT, most of our observations were done within day to day social interactions within our social groups. We simply observed the interactions, both interpersonal and with their smartphones, made by members of our social groups when they were planning a meal out, taking special note of what applications they used and the ways in which they used them.

Survey results

All of our respondents happened to be between 18-25 years old, and 87% of respondents were RMIT students, with the others being other university students or locals, which can be expected as this was by far the most important target demographic of our application. However, this also led to biased results as it meant we did not get any responses from some of the other potential demographics we identified, such as staff. This suggests that either our user groups could have been better distinguished, that our survey methodology focused too much on this particular user group, or that these other demographics are simply not as likely to be interested in using this sort of app and hence also disinterested in completing a survey about one.

        Within this dominant user group, the survey results showed that there were slightly more male than female respondents at 56.3% male. This shows that the use of food apps as well as the issue of healthy eating is relevant to both genders within this demographic. In addition, respondents tended to eat out more often than not, with ‘1-2 days a week’ and ‘almost every day’ making up over 90% of the results. The frequency of eating out could also be linked to being on campus, as over 90% of respondents answered that they were significantly more likely (rating themselves 4 or 5 out of 5, with 5 being very likely) to eat out if they were on campus. Health-related factors were not very important in the respondents’ food choices, with only 26.7% of respondents responding that they were always played a part in their food decisions.

The most important reasons our respondents stated for why they eat out were for the convenience and for social reasons (see Appendix - Survey - eating habits). Only one respondent cited health reasons, lending weight to the argument that outside food is perceived as unhealthy. As predicted, convenience and cost were the biggest deciding factors in where our respondents chose to eat out. This data suggests that aside from incorporating features to assist in healthy eating, our app should incorporate some indication of the cost of a restaurant, as well as its convenience (the latter most likely in the form of a map). The results also showed that the main reasons people would use a dining app would be to discover new places to eat and to browse the available options (see Appendix - Survey - food app habits), with health reasons placing last, meaning our app’s health features must be well integrated with the features expected of a dining app.

When it came to awareness and use of competing apps, only three out of sixteen respondents had used Yelp in their lifetime; Zomato was better represented with eight respondents (see Appendix - Survey - Zomato usage and Appendix - Survey - Yelp usage). Between both of these apps, respondents noted that by far the main reason they downloaded the app was to find new places to eat, and the feature they liked most was the ability to quickly find and read reviews. Despite this, they never used the app in isolation, always using it in conjunction with another method of finding food. The majority of users also did not rely on the in-app navigation features, preferring to use their smartphone’s built-in maps app or a combination of both. Finally, users tended to uninstall the app within a matter of days after installing it, often citing that it was unneeded when other options existed, or in the case of Zomato, their preference to use the web version.

Our observations and interview responses also showed this demographic’s reluctance to use a dedicated food app. The exclusive use of Google or Google Maps, without any other food app (even the web version Zomato) was seen in all of our observations, and Google was also referred to by name by all four of our interview candidates when asked how they usually found food.

These responses point to the market for standalone food apps for our particular target demographic being a very challenging one into which to break, let alone having a healthy eating component which was noted by most respondents as not a priority. This is likely due to the effects of being brought up in an age of developing technology which necessitates a high level of technological literacy, bringing with it an increased proficiency in finding information on the Internet without the need of the extra download of a dedicated app. In addition, tech giants such as Google who offer an all-encompassing, integrated service also take away incentive for consumers to use more specialised competing products when it is much more convenient to stay within the integrated service, as evidenced by the survey responses.

Design Directions

Despite the challenges outlined above, our design direction remains to deliver an app that is essentially a hybrid of a food finder app (ala Zomato) and a diet assistant app, integrating the features of the two, as the survey, interview and observation responses clearly shows that our target audience favours integrated experiences. In addition, since the survey revealed that health is low on the priority list compared to convenience and cost when eating out, an integrated implementation of these features could help sway decisions in favour of healthier ones since the health information would be made easily accessible alongside the other information.

Although users preferred to use external navigation apps, a campus map feature would still be crucial in our app to highlight the location and thus convenience of each food option. As a university campus is not very large geographically, this could even be the main screen of our app, displayed upon loading, to help the user quickly situate themselves in relation to the available food options. In addition, a review and rating system, as well as user-submitted tags and photos, would be implemented as most respondents stated that they downloaded food apps mainly for reviews.

Where our app would differ from most food apps is the inclusion of health-related features, firstly being a ‘health rating’ for each restaurant displayed alongside the main rating with equal prominence. This would be calculated via a combination of user-submitted scores as well as our own research into each restaurants’ menu, and would inform the user at a glance as to whether the option was on the healthier side or not, without having to go to an external source of information. In addition, we would include other health-related features typically found in diet apps, including a calorie counter, with the calorie count of the menu items from restaurants on campus pre-calculated in order to be able to be quickly added to the daily log alongside custom entries. We would also allow the user to track other metrics such as their remaining calorie intake per day, as well as their water intake, and include features such as the location of all water fountains on campus on the map to assist.  All of these features would be represented visually via graphs or other images, as the survey results universally showed that respondents would be more receptive to making changes in their diet if they could have visual feedback as to what they were eating (see Appendix - Survey - diet tracking and diet app feature requirements).

Visually, the application would have to meet minimalistic design standards, not only because it is modern practice but also because some interview candidates responded that the interface of Zomato was a bit too cluttered for their taste, showing that they value clean, distilled interfaces. We would use an existing design framework such as Google’s Material Design in order to make sure our application’s feel remains consistent with what users have been trained to expect. Our main accent colour would be green, as it connotes health and freshness. Restaurant ratings and health ratings would be displayed prominently as the user clicks on them on the map as well as on each restaurant’s own page.

We believe that integrating the health- and diet- related information and features seamlessly alongside more traditional features of dining apps such as reviews and star ratings would encourage users to make healthier choices when eating on campus. This is due to the fact that our user research overwhelmingly showed that the reason that most people didn’t use standalone apps for food was due to a lack of integration and convenience to access certain information. If health information were made as accessible as more traditional information about restaurants, users of our app would have no choice but to notice, even if it will be hard to sway users away from a near-monopoly on information such as Google in the first place.
