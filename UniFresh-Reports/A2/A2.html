Introduction

Eating out is a big part of life on campus, and being able to eat healthily is always a benefit, even though it is usually not the first thing on the mind for students. Through our market research, as presented in our previous report, we have determined that an application which provides healthy eating aids and information closely integrated with more traditional food finder app features is the best way we can approach the brief. This is because our target audience - mostly young, tech-savvy students - was found to highly favour integrated services such as Google in order to find food, as opposed to standalone apps such as Zomato or Yelp.  

To this end, our iOS app, UniFresh, will deliver an integrated experience combining the features of a diet app such as a calorie counter, meal planner and health ratings for each restaurant alongside the features of a more traditional food app including a map, review system and traditional star rating system. This would ensure that health-related information is right at the fingertips of the consumer at every step - where the target audience expects it to be if it is to be read at all. By making health-related information highly visible, we hope that we can embed the idea of health conscious eating into our users’ minds and decision making, even if they never actually deliberately interact with any of the health related features.

Context Scenarios

Lilly

Task: Order food for pickup

Lilly has just finished a lecture and has a 3 hour break till her next class. She decides she wants to do some study in the library while she waits, but not before she grabs some food first. She isn’t totally familiar with the campus yet and normally brings lunch from home so she decides to download a campus food app and check out what’s on there. She finds a place with a good health rating and the menu looks good to her so she decides to place an order so it’s ready when she gets there, after a couple of clicks her food is ordered and paid for and she walks down to the cafe.

John

Task: Plan calorie intake for the day

John has just started his lunch break for today and wants to check his calorie intake for today as he had to skip the gym this morning due to unforeseen traffic. He opens the app he downloaded last week, to help him track his calories, and navigates to the health page. As it loads up he sees that he forgot to add his breakfast this morning. He adds bacon and eggs to his page specifying how much of each; the app then calculates the calories and displays his daily total thus far. John remembers that he is going to an Indian restaurant for dinner tonight for his brother's birthday. He adds in an approximation of wait he might eat later and looks to see how many calories that will likely be. After noticing he might surpass the daily intake for today if he goes out for lunch, he decides to skip it. He closes the app and heads back to the office.

Yukari

Task: Find a restaurant in close proximity in a short amount of time

Yukari has just finished tutoring a class at 7:30pm on a Thursday night. She has a 9 am start the next day so needs to go home soon, but wants to eat as she has not had time to do so in the last 7 hours. Yukari isn’t sure what is open at this time of night on a Thursday. She opens the app (which she had previously installed) and takes a look at the map, which shows markers displaying the restaurants which are open in her area; the markers are also coloured according to health rating. She taps the closest marker, which has a high health rating meaning it is likely not as heavy as other choices, and reveals more information about the restaurant. Seeing that it has a high star rating and is only 2 minutes walk away, she decides to walk to the restaurant to view the menu board, order and pay in person.

A1

A1b: app splash screen

Consistency and standards: the app icon and splash screen are consistently featured in other apps for iPhone.

Visibility of system status: a splash screen indicates that the app is being opened. Modern apps load fast enough that there is no need for a loading wheel or loading bar (this is consistent across other apps), therefore including one would introduce unnecessary clutter.

Aesthetic and minimalistic design: the splash screen shows only the wordmark of the app, avoiding clutter as stated above.

A2

Aesthetic and minimalistic design: there are minimal buttons on the main screen of the app; menu items are hidden until needed.

Bottom navigation: the main buttons, “dining” and “health”, are positioned near the bottom of the screen easily accessible by the thumbs.

Sidebar navigation: the options and profile menus will slide in from the side when selected.

Recognition rather than recall: the profile, search and options buttons use recognisable icons consistent with those in other apps.

A3

She swaps to list view by clicking on the title bar on the Healthy category

Filmstrip design: different restaurants in each category on this screen are positioned beside each other.

Match between system and real world: recommended restaurants are displayed by category like on a menu and in other apps. Photos of the food offered at each restaurant are displayed instead of restaurant logos.

Colour and contrast: our health ratings on this screen will be brighter green the higher it is. Green is a colour usually associated with health as well as being beneficial / good.

Vertical stack design: there are minimal side elements present in all menus.

Application Features

The features in our application were determined based on the results of the user survey, as well as features present in other apps.

Map and search bar

The home screen of the application is a map, showing markers of restaurants near the campus. This was chosen in order to quickly show users the possible food choices near them, as 94% of survey respondents stated that convenience was one of the main reasons they ate out, and 70% responded that it was one of the main factors in choosing an establishment at which to eat out. On top of this, 50% of Zomato users surveyed stated that they did not use the inbuilt map at all, prompting us to put our map in a more central location. We decided that a map being the home screen would not overload the user with information, as a university campus is likely to be small and not have enough food options to cause information overload. In addition, the map could help with general navigation.

The search bar is present in multiple screens and allows the user to immediately search for a food option, alongside other health related amenities on campus such as water taps. Searching allows the user to bypass the need to click through multiple category menus if they already know what they are looking for.

Review system and star and health ratings

63% of the Zomato users we surveyed reported the review functions being the main reason they used the app. A review system allows users to share their experience at each establishment, meaning that other users can get unbiased opinions that are not from the restaurant itself. A star rating system is common across a vast majority of hospitality apps and reviews and is a quick way for users to tell the reputation of an establishment at a glance. We decided to expand on the traditional star rating system by using the same system to rate the healthiness of an establishment and placing this information in proximity to the usual star rating, as 82% of survey respondents stated that having a visual representation of the healthiness of what they chose to eat would influence their eating choices.

In-app ordering

This feature also stems from wanting to provide the maximum amount of convenience to users due to the extreme emphasis on this factor in their dining choices. With this feature, users can pay through the app to order a meal ahead of time so it is ready when they arrive at the establishment. Time savings like these are important to the target audience as university students are usually strapped for time.

We intentionally decided not to include a delivery service as the target audience is specifically people who are already on campus, meaning that most of the food options they choose should be within walking distance. In addition, it would be hard to implement a concise interface to specify delivery to a specific room of the university (as would be expected for an app aimed at those on campus), and for delivery drivers, who would likely not be familiar with the campus, to find the location in a timely manner.

Integrated meal planner and calorie counter

These features are offered to users through the prominently displayed “health” button alongside the “dining” button. 100% of survey respondents stated that it would be convenient to know how much they should eat during a certain time frame, and 95% responded that it would be easier to adhere to an eating plan if there was a visual representation. Thus, the most prominent element on our “health” screen is a large graph displaying the calorie intake for the day, which changes colour as the user approaches their limit. From this, users are able to select the items they have ordered through the app that day to add to each meal in the tracker, as well as pre plan their meals using items from the restaurants on offer within the app. Of course, users are also able to input custom items and meals.

In addition, these features are also prominent throughout the dining sections of the app. Calorie counts for each item are displayed alongside their cost when ordering or viewing the menu. The checkout screen also displays the total calorie count of the order, and when the user places an order, the calorie count will automatically be added to that day’s counter, with an option to go directly to the health screen after the order is complete. This all helps to increase the visibility of health related information to the user, and help inform them as to how to make healthier choices.
